The heritage marker text reads:

From 1978-1981 the streets of Macarthur made motorcycling history.

In the late 1970s, Macarthur’s vacant residential blocks and empty streets caught the imagination of the newly-formed Canberra Road Racing Club (CRRC). The suburb’s natural amphitheatre, perfect road surfaces and modern spoon-shaped drains were the ingredients for a dream road racing circuit!

In 1978 the National capital Development Commission (NCDC) gave permission for the CRRC to conduct the first of a series of motorcycle race meetings, and ‘Macarthur Park’ as the area became known, came to life.

The first meeting attracted national media attention and an estimated 10,000 spectators – 3,000 people watched the practice sessions!

Macarthur Park was a unique experience for everyone who rode there. Well-known racers of that era – and racers who went on to achieve national and international status in the motorcycling world – diced alongside club riders from ACT, Victoria and NSW.

The CRRC held other informal events between 1978-81, and the circuit was also used for kart racing and as a stage on the Castrol International Rally. By 1983 the first houses appeared and Macarthur Park became a memory.

Today the only clue to this suburb’s spectacular racing past is a small section of missing median strip at the Coyne Street intersection. Removed to allow for sidecar racing in 1981, it was never replaced.

* Note for non-Australians:
ACT = Australian Capital Territory – the area around Canberra.
NSW = New South Wales.

The story is partly told on the marker, but there are a couple of other angles that are not mentioned.  The reason the suburb was available for so long was that in the mid to late 70s Canberra's population suddenly stopped growing for a while.  The government did not react quickly enough, so the suburb-building program got ahead of demand, and there were three or four suburbs out here with nobody to move into them.   It was a great place for teaching kids to drive!  The bikers cut a deal with the government to put Macarthur at the bottom of the list for settlement, so they had four or five years to run their races.  Eventually, however, the demand picked up again and the Government needed Macarthur.  It was not a case of people who don't like noise stampeding to build their houses near a circuit.  The suburb was intended to be built on from the beginning, and that's why the roads were built.  It was a small mercy that some races were able to be run before the inevitable houses arrived.
