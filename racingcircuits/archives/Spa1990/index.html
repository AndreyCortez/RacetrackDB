The Spa region has been associated with motor sport since the very early years of racing. The first motor sport event in Belgium was organised by the Automobile Club de Belgique in Spa with the ‘Concours d'Automobiles’ meeting which was held in 1896. Two years later saw the first city to city race from Brussels to Spa. A year afterwards, races were run from Brussels via Namur to Spa, followed a few days later with a race from Spa to Brussels and a back to Spa. These early races were influential in inspiring the ‘Circuit des Ardennes’ races from 1902 to 1907, which took place on a triangular circuit of closed public roads from Bastogne via Longlier to Habay-la-Neuve and back to Bastogne. In 1906 a city to city race was organised from Spa to Luxembourg and back to the town of Malchamps, which is located just to the north of the current Grand Prix circuit.

The Spa-Francorchamps circuit was established in August 1921. Conceived by Jules de Their and Henri Langlois Van Ophem, the course consisted of 9.3 miles of fast and undulating tree-lined public roads between the towns of Francorchamps, Malmedy and Stavelot. The circuit was demanding, with many challenging, high speed corners. The first event was a motorcycle race which took place in 1921. The first car races began in 1922 and it wasn’t long before the circuit hosted international level competition, starting in 1924 with the first Spa 24 Hours followed by the Belgian Grand Prix in 1925.

The first incarnation of the circuit featured a sharp left turn at a bridge over the Eau Rouge river immediately after the start/finish straight, which led the drivers around the Ancienne Douanne loop, with the cars returning to Eau Rouge before climbing up the hill up to Les Combes. The circuit then plunged downhill for a flat-out blast through the quick and demanding Burnenville corner and through the curve at Malmedy. Cars then ran at full speed along the valley bottom, sweeping through the Masta Kink, an unforgiving quick left-right sequence with no margin for error. After another straight they flicked right-left at Holowell, which led them into the hairpin at Stavelot. At this point the circuit began to climb back up hill towards Francorchamps, passing through some incredibly quick corners at La Carriére and Blanchimont, before heavy braking was required for the tight right hand hairpin at La Source to complete the lap.

For the 1939 race the circuit was modified, the cars turning directly uphill at Eau Rouge with the removal of the Ancienne Douanne section. After the end of the war, a new banked link road was also established at Stavelot, which resulted in an increase in the average speed over the course of a lap.

Because of the length of the circuit and the changeable local weather conditions, the Spa circuit would become notorious for its unpredictability. Frequently, one section of the circuit would be dry, whilst another would be deluged by a rainstorm. Combined with the numerous trackside hazards, the slippery surface was extremely dangerous for the competitors. Bill Holowell crashed fatally on the run down to Stavelot whilst in third place on his 350cc AJS during the 1925 TT. In 1939 Dick Seaman crashed out of the lead of the wet Grand Prix race in his Mercedes-Benz whilst negotiating the fast left curve before La Source. His car hit a tree sideways on, and the driver was trapped unconscious inside the burning wreckage. He would later die of his injuries. During the 1958 Grand Prix of Spa for Sportscars, Archie Scott-Brown’s Lister-Jaguar also crashed in the same area after hitting a marker stone. He too received severe burns from which he tragically died.

The 1960 Belgian Grand Prix would be the among the blackest weekends of the circuit’s history. During practice, Mike Taylor suffered extensive injuries after crashing his Lotus into a tree and Stirling Moss broke both his legs when he was thrown from his Lotus following an axle failure. During the race Chris Bristow lost control of his Cooper at Burnenville and was decapitated after sliding off into a fence. Five laps later Alan Stacy was hit in the face by a bird near Masta and died when his Lotus left the road and crashed heavily.

A year later Cliff Allison rolled his Lotus and suffered career ending injuries to his legs. During the 1962 event, Trevor Taylor’s Lotus tangled with Willie Mairesse’s Ferrari just after Blanchimont, with Mairesse suffering severe burns. Lancia driver Piero Frescobaldi suffered a fatal accident during the 1964 Spa 24 Hours. The 1966 Belgian Grand Prix featured a heavy downpour just after the start, which eliminated half the field in the opening lap. Jackie Stewart lost control of his BRM at the Masta Kink and was trapped in his overturned car, which was leaking fuel. He suffered a broken shoulder and ribs, and also internal bruising. It was an experience that would prove influential in his quest to improve safety in Formula 1. At Blanchimont a year later, Mike Parkes spun on oil and rolled his Ferrari several times. He was thrown from the car and sustained massive leg injuries, which would end his racing career. This followed the tragic deaths of Wim Loos and Eric De Keyn who each crashed their Alfa Romeo’s during the 24 Hour race. The 1969 running of the 24 Hours would see the fatality of Leon Dernier following an accident in his Mazda R 100.

In 1970 a chicane was introduced at Malmedy for the Grand Prix cars in an effort to improve driver safety. However, this would prove to be the last Belgian Grand Prix held on the original Spa circuit, as it was decided that it had become too dangerous for Formula 1. The series would instead race at the featureless Nivelles-Baulers in 1972 and 1974, before settling for a home at Zolder. In the meantime however, the Spa 24 Hours would continue to be held on the circuit. The 1971 event saw the death of BMW driver Raymond Mathay, whilst 1973 saw the deaths of drivers Massimo Larini, Hans-Peter Joisten and Roger Dubos. Wim Boshuis crashed fatally in his Opel Commodore during the 1975 running of the 24 Hour race.

In 1979 a new link road was built connecting the circuit from Les Combes to point on the return road from Stavelot to Blanchimont. This significantly reduced the length of the circuit and was a great improvement in terms of safety. With the introduction of the Bus Stop chicane at the old Clubhouse corner in 1981, Formula 1 returned for the 1983 season and would finally become the venue for Grand Prix from 1985 and beyond.
